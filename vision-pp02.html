PP02

Leadership Through Consensus

Protecting democratic decision-making in Collingwood.

Why This Matters

Ontario has extended Strong Mayor powers to designated municipalities, including Collingwood. Those powers give the Mayor additional statutory authorities in defined areas. They do not remove Council, public debate or professional administration. The question for Collingwood is not whether the powers exist — it is how they should be exercised.

Our Vision

Strong leadership strengthens democracy; it does not replace it. Residents elect a Mayor, a Deputy Mayor and seven Councillors from diverse perspectives. The Mayor’s responsibility is to lead that team toward the community’s long-term interests — earning the confidence of Council and residents by building consensus.

Guiding Principle: The strongest Mayor is not the one with the greatest statutory authority, but the one who earns the confidence of Council and the community by building consensus.

1. Lead Through Consensus

Good government is measured by the wisdom, transparency and accountability of its decisions — not only by how quickly decisions are made.

2. Strengthen Democracy

Additional mayoral tools should support collaborative Council decision-making, open debate and accountable administration — not replace them.

3. Choose How Power Is Used

Where the law permits, authority should be shared and delegated. Leadership is choosing when to lead alone, and when to lead together.

First Edition — July 2026. Vision for Collingwood: Public Policy Paper No. 2.

Understanding the Issue

What changed — and how Strong Mayor powers should be used

Understanding the Issue

What Changed

The Province of Ontario has extended Strong Mayor powers based on population criteria, including for Collingwood. The framework provides the Mayor with additional authorities in defined areas while Council, debate and administration continue to matter.

The Principle

Where provincial legislation permits, available authorities should be delegated to Council or entrusted to the Chief Administrative Officer whenever doing so preserves balance, transparency and better outcomes for residents.

The Leadership Question

The question is not whether Strong Mayor tools exist. It is how — and when — they should be used so Collingwood continues to govern in a way that reflects its democratic character within that legislative framework.

Why This Matters

  1. Preserves Balance Recognizes diverse Council perspectives while respecting mayoral leadership.
  2. Protects Transparency Keeps decisions visible, with clear reasons the public can understand.
  3. Builds Better Outcomes Collaboration brings more knowledge to the table and more resilient results.
  4. Maintains Resident Trust Residents expect their elected representatives to work together.
  5. Ensures Checks and Balances Avoids concentrating power in a single office when sharing is available.
  6. Supports Sound Judgment Strong leadership is about choosing when — and when not — to use special tools.
  7. Puts Community First Collingwood thrives when leadership is shared and our community comes first.

Decision Areas Under Strong Mayor Powers

Budget Authority

How the municipal budget is proposed, advanced and ultimately approved within the legislative framework.

Planning, Housing & Infrastructure

How major planning, housing and infrastructure priorities move from proposal to Council decision.

Veto Power Over By-laws

When mayoral veto tools may apply, and how Council can respond under the Strong Mayor rules.

CAO & Senior Administration

How the Chief Administrative Officer and senior organizational leadership are appointed and directed.

Note: Most Strong Mayor powers relate to provincially prescribed priorities and should be used with clear public reasons whenever they are exercised.

The Plan

How Strong Mayor powers should be used — a leadership choice

The Plan

01

Collaboration First

Start with Council, staff, stakeholders and residents. Collective decision-making remains Collingwood’s greatest strength.

02

Respect for Council

Elected Councillors bring diverse perspectives that strengthen outcomes. Leadership means seeking and considering those views.

03

Respect for Every Resident

Every resident deserves a voice in local democracy. Decisions should reflect the long-term interests of the whole community.

04

Clear Communication

Explain the issue, the options and the reasons for a decision in plain language the public can follow.

05

Results That Matter

Use tools only when they advance durable, practical outcomes — with accountability for the choices that are made.

06

Use Powers Sparingly

Even when appropriate, Strong Mayor powers should be used with humility, restraint and a plan for ongoing engagement.

When May Using a Strong Mayor Power Be Appropriate?

Urgent Needs

Time-sensitive decisions where delay would clearly harm residents, services or statutory deadlines.

Provincial Priorities

Actions tied to prescribed provincial housing, infrastructure or growth priorities under the Strong Mayor framework.

Council Gridlock

Situations where genuine deadlock prevents progress on a matter that serves the broader community interest.

Community Benefit

When a measured use of the tool will clearly and accountably advance Collingwood’s long-term public interest.

Even when appropriate, these powers should be used sparingly, with humility, and always with a plan for ongoing engagement and accountability.

Decision Test Before Using a Strong Mayor Power

Community Interest

Will using this power clearly advance the long-term interests of Collingwood?

Consensus

Have I genuinely sought and considered the views of Council, staff, stakeholders and residents?

Necessity

Is using this power necessary — and is it the most proportionate response?

Transparency

Can I explain this decision clearly to the public — and be held accountable for it?

Community Benefit

Will residents clearly benefit — especially where action is urgent or Council is gridlocked?

If the answer is “no” to any of these tests, leadership means choosing not to use the power.

What This Means for Residents

  • Collingwood elects nine independent representatives — not one.
  • Strong Mayor tools should support collaboration, not replace Council judgment.
  • Where the law allows, authorities should be shared and decisions explained openly.
  • Any use of special powers should be rare, necessary and accountable to the public.

Policy Position

Where the Province gives authority, the Mayor still chooses how that authority is used. The right choice for Collingwood is to delegate what can be delegated, build consensus, and reserve Strong Mayor tools for moments when they clearly serve the community interest.

What Success Looks Like

My first act as Mayor — leadership through consensus

What Success Looks Like

Measuring Success

  • Delegable authorities are shared with Council or entrusted to the CAO where the law permits
  • Major decisions continue through open discussion and collective Council judgment
  • Any Strong Mayor power that is used is explained publicly with clear reasons
  • Residents see collaboration, transparency and accountability in practice
  • Administrative leadership remains professional and clearly defined
  • Collingwood’s democratic character is strengthened within the provincial framework

First Act / First 100 Days

At the first meeting of the new Council, bring a resolution affirming collaborative governance: delegate what can be delegated, entrust administrative authorities to the CAO, keep decision-making centred on Council, and commit to transparency whenever a Strong Mayor power is used.

Deliverable: A public Council resolution and governance statement setting out which authorities will be shared, how decisions will be explained, and how accountability will be maintained.

Closing Message

The Province determines what powers a Mayor possesses. A Mayor determines how those powers will be exercised. Collingwood’s future will be strongest when we combine the tools of the Strong Mayor framework with the timeless values of collaboration, respect and accountability.

Leadership is a choice. Consensus is a commitment.

— Norm
